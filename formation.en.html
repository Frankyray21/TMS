Risk factors at work

Force

Heavy loads, intense efforts.

Repetition

The same movements, over and over.

Awkward postures

Bent back, raised arms, twisting.

Vibrations

Vibrating tools and machines.

Cold and damp

Makes tissues less flexible.

Static postures

Staying frozen in place for a long time.

For workers, the risk comes mostly from the task, the body and the immediate environment.

🔍 Zoom in

An MSD doesn't have a single cause: it's a mix of effort, posture, repetition, fatigue and environment. Acting early helps keep the problem from setting in.

Underground, the risk adds up

Long shifts

Fatigue builds up, and alertness drops at the end of the shift.

Manual handling

Rods, hoses and heavy equipment often moved by hand.

Tight spaces

Working bent over, crouched or kneeling, in positions you can't choose.

Uneven ground

Unstable footing, extra strain on the back and knees.

The silent progression

Symptoms show up gradually. By the time the condition is fully set in, it's already late to step in.

1

Discomfort

  • Pain while working, going away with rest
  • Localized fatigue, on-and-off discomfort
  • Stiffness, tension at the end of the shift
2

Pain

  • Shows up early in the workday and lasts longer
  • Takes more time to go away
  • Disrupted sleep, reduced output
3

Injury

  • Persistent pain during activity and at rest
  • Problems that last for weeks
  • Major loss of function
⚠

The right time to act is at stage 1. The longer you wait, the longer and less certain the recovery.

Microtears, the mechanism behind wear and tear

Repeated muscle contractions create microtears in the muscle and tendon fibres, which triggers an inflammatory response. What happens next depends on recovery time:

Enough rest

Muscles get stronger and tendons more resistant.

Not enough rest

Chronic breakdown of the tissues.

Recovery time decides whether the tissues adapt or break down. — Docking et Cook, 2019

Pain becoming chronic

Pain gets worse over time — act as soon as you feel discomfort
Window for actionAcute0 to 6 weeks
Subacute6 to 12 weeks
Chronic> 12 weeks (3+ months)
The longer the pain lasts, the longer the recovery. Chronic pain is rare but has a poor outlook: it needs proper care.

Part 02

Workload and recovery

An MSD shows up when the load put on the body repeatedly outpaces its ability to recover.

What increases the load

  • Force, heavy loads
  • Repeated movements, long hours
  • Awkward postures, vibrations
  • Cold, stress, fast pace

What restores your capacity

  • Enough good-quality sleep
  • Hydration and food
  • Physical activity, mobility
  • Breaks, managing stress

Part 03

Sleep

Your body's number one repair crew. While you sleep, your body repairs itself.

Tissue repair

Muscles, tendons and joints rebuild themselves mostly at night.

Recovery hormones

Growth hormone, released in deep sleep, repairs tissues.

Managing pain

Good sleep lowers your sensitivity to pain and inflammation.

Brain and alertness

Memory and focus get locked in. You stay sharp on the next shift.

7-9h /24h

The recommended sleep target

You can't make up for fatigue with one sleep-in. It builds up like a debt.

Lack of sleep = more risk

When you're short on sleep, your whole body pays the price.

⚠ What gets worse

  • •Slower reaction time
  • •Reduced focus and judgment
  • •Pain felt more strongly
  • •Incomplete muscle recovery
  • •More wrong moves and accidents

Fatigue affects you like alcohol

Staying awake 17 to 19 hours straight affects your alertness as much as a blood alcohol level of 0.05.

Your body isn't naturally built to sleep during the day. These habits help you recover better between shifts.

Dark, cool room

Blackout curtains, earplugs, a mask. Darkness mimics night.

Set routine

Same ritual before sleep, even if it's morning.

Caffeine under control

No coffee in the 6 hours before your planned sleep.

Strategic nap

A short 20 to 30 minute nap before a night shift.

Light when you wake up

Get bright light to kick your alertness back up.

Protect your sleep

Let the people around you know, turn off notifications.

Do

  • Aim for 7 to 9 hours of sleep per 24 hours
  • Keep regular bedtimes
  • Cut off screens 30 to 60 minutes before
  • Cool, dark and quiet room
  • Move during the day, not right before bed

Avoid

  • Alcohol to fall asleep
  • Coffee at the end of your shift (and in the 6 hours before sleep)
  • Heavy meals before sleep
Part 04

Healthy living

Four everyday levers: water, food, movement and habits.

A dehydrated muscle tires faster, gets stiffer and cramps more easily. Underground, in the heat and with the effort, you lose a lot of water without realizing it. Dehydration cuts your focus and raises the risk of a wrong move.

Drink before you're thirsty

Thirst is already a sign of dehydration.

Keep a water bottle at your work station

Within reach, you drink more often.

Watch the colour

Clear urine = well hydrated. Dark = drink more.

Food is fuel.

Steady energy

Complete meals: whole grains, vegetables, protein. They prevent energy crashes mid-shift.

Protein to repair

Meat, fish, eggs, legumes: the building blocks that rebuild the muscles you've worked.

Limit fast sugar

Sugary drinks and snacks: a quick boost followed by a crash. Up-and-down alertness.

Eat anti-inflammatory

Fruits, vegetables and fatty fish help your body handle the inflammation that comes with exertion.

Move and stay mobile. A strong, flexible body handles the workload better. Movement outside of work is part of prevention.

Strengthen your core and back

A strong back and abs protect your spine while you handle loads.

Stay flexible

Flexible muscles move through a greater range without getting hurt.

Beat sitting still

Walk and move on your days off. Sitting for long stretches stiffens your body too.

Tobacco, alcohol, cannabis: their effects on recovery.

Tobacco

Cuts blood flow to your tissues, so the muscles and tendons you've worked heal more slowly.

Alcohol

Helps you fall asleep but breaks up your deep sleep. Less repair, more fatigue the next day.

Cannabis

Affects your alertness, coordination and reaction time. The effect can last several hours.

Part 05

Good habits at your work station

What you can put into practice as soon as tomorrow to protect your back, your shoulders and your joints.

🔍 Zoom in

Load close to your body, respect the natural curves of your back, pivot with your feet, work at the right height: good postures reduce the pressure on your spine, your muscles and your joints.

🔍 Zoom in

Load far from your body, rounded back under effort, twisting your trunk and reaching with your arms for too long: the postures that add pressure on your spine.

0°Comfort zone
0-20° comfort20-45° watch out>45° awkward

Slide it to simulate the angle of your arm relative to your body. The bigger the angle, the more strain on your shoulder. Past 60° held for a while, the risk goes way up.

🔍 Zoom in

Shoulders, wrists and hands: the further the position gets from neutral, the more the strain goes up. 0 to 20 degrees = comfort, 20 to 45 = watch out, more than 45 = awkward.

Get your body ready before the shift

A cold muscle gets hurt more easily. 5 minutes of warm-up beats weeks off work.

1

Loosen up your joints

  • Neck, shoulders, wrists, hips, knees
  • A few gentle rotations
2

Wake up your back and legs

  • Light squats, lunges
  • Lower-back stretches
3

Build up the intensity

  • Start out easy
  • Let your body warm up before the heavy work

Micro-breaks and rotation

Switch up your postures

Alternate between standing, crouching and moving. No posture is good for too long.

Micro-breaks

A few seconds to let your muscles relax before they tense up.

Rotate your tasks

When you can, change up the movement to rest the areas you've been using.

Quick stretches

A few targeted stretches through the shift keep your muscles loose.

Listen to the signals, report early

A discomfort that keeps coming back isn't a weakness to hide. It's information to share fast.

Why report early

  • ›An MSD caught early heals fast
  • ›The work station or the task can be adjusted
  • ›You keep it from turning chronic
  • ›Your report protects your co-workers

Who to talk to

  • ›Your immediate supervisor
  • ›The health and safety advisor or committee
  • ›The site first-aiders if needed
  • ›Don't wait for it to get worse

Pillar 1: Reduce the load

  • Good handling techniques
  • Warm-up and micro-breaks
  • Vary your postures and tasks
  • Mechanical aids, ask for help

Pillar 2: Maximize recovery

  • 7 to 9 hours of sleep, on a regular schedule
  • Stay well hydrated, eat well
  • Move, keep your strength and flexibility
  • Habits that help your body repair

Sleep

  • Fixed bedtime
  • Dark, cool room
  • No coffee at the end of your shift

Healthy living

  • Water bottle at your work station every day
  • A snack that holds you over
  • Move 20 min on a day off

At your work station

  • 5 min of warm-up
  • Bend your knees, no twisting
  • Report any discomfort
